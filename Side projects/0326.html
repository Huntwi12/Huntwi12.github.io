Cleveland-Cliffs (CLF) underwent a dramatic transformation from 2020–2025: it began as a pure iron ore pellet producer, then became North America's largest flat-rolled steel producer through two landmark acquisitions — AK Steel (March 2020) and ArcelorMittal USA (December 2020). It later acquired Stelco Holdings (November 2024). This period spans a full cycle: COVID disruption → steel supercycle peak → pricing collapse → tariff recovery.

Key Earnings Drivers by Era
2020 — Transformation Under Fire
COVID-19 pandemic hit just as AK Steel acquisition closed. Auto plants shut, steel demand cratered. ArcelorMittal USA acquisition added massive scale in Dec 2020. Full year net loss, but Q4 showed recovery potential.
2021 — Steel Supercycle Peak
HRC steel prices surged from ~$700/ton to ~$1,900/ton. First full year as an integrated steel giant. Record quarterly results in Q2, Q3, Q4. EPS of $5.36 — the best in company history. $1B buyback launched.
2022 — Record Revenue, Fading Prices
Full-year revenue hit a record $23B. Russia-Ukraine war initially boosted prices, but HRC fell from ~$1,400 to ~$600 by year-end. Q3 and Q4 missed estimates as margins compressed. $1.8B pension liability reduction.
2023 — Navigating the Downturn
Steel prices stabilized at lower levels. Strong Q3 beat on record auto shipments (despite UAW strike impact in Q3–Q4). Long-term automotive contracts provided pricing floor. Net debt fell to ~$2.9B. EPS $0.78 GAAP.
2024 — Imports & Stelco Drag
Import competition and weak HRC prices (~$700/ton avg) crushed margins. Stelco acquisition closed Nov 2024 adding ~C$3.3B in consideration and restructuring charges. Missed estimates in all 4 quarters. Net loss of $708M GAAP.
2025 — Tariffs & Restructuring
Worst full year on record. Q1 devastating before Trump's 25% Section 232 tariffs (March 2025) took effect. H2 showed improvement as tariffs curbed imports. Q4 beat estimates. Asset sales and cost cuts reduced net debt to ~$1.3B.

What Drives CLF's Performance
🏭 HRC Steel Prices
The single biggest driver. CLF's avg selling price tracks HRC steel with a lag (automotive contracts buffer short-term swings). Every ~$100/ton change in HRC = ~$1.2–1.5B impact on revenue annually given ~15M ton shipment volumes.
🚗 Automotive Contracts
~50–60% of CLF's shipments are to automotive OEMs (Ford, GM, Stellantis, etc.) under annual fixed-price contracts. This creates a 1-year lag vs. spot prices — great in a down market, a drag in a rapid rise. Record auto shipments in Q3 2023 demonstrated this anchor.
🌍 Steel Imports
Import competition (especially from Asia, Turkey, and MENA) is the primary threat to domestic steel pricing. Section 232 tariffs under Trump (25%) were the most effective protection. Biden's tariff relaxations contributed to the 2024 pricing collapse.
⚡ Operating Leverage
CLF's blast furnace + integrated model has very high fixed costs (~$650–700/ton). When HRC is $1,500/ton, margins are extraordinary. When HRC is $650/ton, the company loses money. This is why EBITDA went from $5.3B (2021) to $37M (2025) on only a 9% revenue decline from peak.
🔄 Acquisitions & Debt
Each major acquisition (AK Steel, AM USA, Stelco) added scale but also debt and integration costs. The 2021 steel supercycle came at the perfect time — profits funded rapid deleveraging. The 2024 Stelco acquisition at $2.4B USD came at a market trough, adding debt stress in an already difficult environment.
🛡️ Trade Policy
CLF's profitability is highly correlated with US trade policy. Trump's Section 232 tariffs (first imposed 2018, re-expanded March 2025) created the conditions for the 2021 supercycle and the 2025 recovery. Biden's tariff exceptions allowed the 2024 import surge that drove losses.

CLF enters 2026 at an inflection point. The 2025 cycle trough appears to be behind them — Q4 2025 beat estimates, net debt has been cut to ~$1.3B, and Section 232 tariffs are intact. But structural headwinds from EAF competition, the EV transition, and a high fixed-cost base mean the road to sustained profitability is not guaranteed. Below is an honest assessment of both sides.

2026–2027 Scenarios
🟢 Bull Case — Tariff-Driven Recovery
EPS Target: $1.50–$2.50
25% Section 232 tariffs remain in place, suppressing imports. HRC prices recover to $900–$1,000/ton. Stelco integration delivers synergies. 2026 automotive contracts re-priced upward. Net debt continues falling. EBITDA could recover to $2.0–2.5B — approaching 2022 levels. The operating leverage that crushed CLF on the way down becomes a powerful tailwind.
🟡 Base Case — Gradual Stabilization
EPS Target: $0.00–$1.00
HRC stabilizes around $800–$850/ton. Tariffs hold but don't dramatically boost pricing further. CLF returns to narrow profitability as cost cuts and Stelco synergies offset a soft demand backdrop. EBITDA recovers to $1.0–1.5B. Beat-or-miss quarters are roughly split. Debt paydown continues at a slower pace. A modest recovery — not a supercycle.
🔴 Bear Case — Policy Reversal or Recession
EPS Target: -$1.00 to -$2.00
A US–trade-partner deal relaxes Section 232 tariffs, imports flood back in. Or a US recession softens auto production and construction. HRC falls back below $700/ton. CLF returns to loss-making territory. The ~$7.3B gross debt load becomes a serious liquidity concern without EBITDA to service it. Potential for asset sales, equity dilution, or covenant pressures.
The Bull Case in Detail
🛡️ Section 232 Tariffs Holding
The 25% steel tariffs reinstated in March 2025 are the single most important near-term driver. They immediately cut import competitiveness and triggered the H2 2025 EBITDA recovery from -$174M to +$80M in two quarters. If they remain — as politically expected under the current administration — CLF has a structural pricing floor it lacked in 2024.
⚡ Operating Leverage, Upward
CLF's blast furnace model has massive fixed costs — but those are already being paid. Every incremental dollar of HRC price improvement above their breakeven (~$750–780/ton) flows almost entirely to the bottom line. A $100/ton HRC improvement from $800 to $900/ton could add ~$1.2–1.5B in annual EBITDA on roughly flat volumes. That's the math that made 2021 extraordinary.
🏗️ Infrastructure & Reshoring Demand
The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) and CHIPS Act continue to generate multi-year demand for domestic steel — bridges, highways, semiconductor fabs, data centers. CLF is a direct beneficiary as the largest domestic flat-rolled and specialty steel producer. This provides a structural demand floor independent of auto cycles.
🔧 Stelco Synergies Coming
The Stelco acquisition was painful in 2024–2025 (acquisition costs, integration charges, bad timing). But Stelco's Hamiltion, Ontario and Lake Erie Works facilities give CLF Canadian production scale, CUSMA-advantaged trade flows, and ~2.2M additional tons of capacity. Synergy realization in 2026 could add $200–400M to annual EBITDA once integration noise clears.
💰 Debt Reduction Momentum
Net debt fell from ~$2.4B (end 2024) to ~$1.3B (end 2025) — a $1.1B reduction in one year, driven by asset sales. A recovering EBITDA profile in 2026 ($1.0–2.0B) would bring the net leverage ratio below 1.5x, a manageable level that removes the balance sheet risk overhang and could support resumed share buybacks.
🚗 Automotive Resilience
Despite EV headwinds, CLF's automotive customers (Ford, GM, Stellantis, Toyota, Honda) are still producing ~15–16M vehicles per year in North America. CLF supplies advanced high-strength steel grades that EVs actually need more of (structural battery enclosures, body-in-white lightweighting). The mix shift from ICE to EV is a gradual transition — not a cliff.
The Bear Case & Key Risks
⚠️ EAF Competition — Structural, Not Cyclical
Nucor, Steel Dynamics, and NLMK USA are all expanding EAF capacity specifically targeting flat-rolled and automotive-grade steel — CLF's core market. EAF mills have ~$150–200/ton cost advantage over blast furnaces, can flex capacity up/down faster, and produce lower-carbon steel (increasingly valued by OEMs). This competitive pressure will not go away regardless of tariffs.
🚗 EV Transition Risk
EVs use ~200 lbs less steel per vehicle than ICE cars (no engine block, transmission, complex stampings). By 2030, if EVs reach 30–40% of new US auto sales, total automotive steel demand could decline 10–15%. CLF's heavy auto exposure (~55% of shipments) is a structural risk. Aluminum substitution in body panels is an additional long-term headwind.
📜 Trade Policy Single-Point Risk
CLF's 2026 profitability hinges significantly on tariffs remaining in place. Any trade negotiation that relaxes Section 232 steel tariffs — even partially, as the Biden administration did with EU, UK, and Japan "TRQ" arrangements — could re-open the import flood. CLF has essentially no control over this. A single executive order could swing annual EBITDA by $1B+.
💸 Gross Debt Load
While net debt has been reduced by asset sales, CLF's gross long-term debt remains ~$7.3B. Interest expense alone is ~$400–500M/year. In a scenario where EBITDA stays below $1B, interest coverage becomes thin and covenant pressure could force dilutive equity raises or additional asset sales at unfavorable prices. The balance sheet is cleaner but not clean.
🌍 China Overcapacity Overhang
China produces ~1 billion tons of steel per year against domestic demand of ~800M tons — a chronic 200M ton surplus that gets exported globally. Even with US tariffs, this surplus depresses global HRC benchmarks and puts constant downward pressure on US prices through indirect routes (e.g., steel pipes, components, transshipment through third countries). There is no near-term resolution to Chinese overcapacity.
🔬 Carbon Transition Costs
Blast furnaces emit ~2 tons of CO₂ per ton of steel. CLF has announced plans to incorporate hydrogen-based direct reduced iron (DRI) technology at its Toledo facility — which could dramatically reduce emissions but requires billions in capex. As carbon border adjustment mechanisms (like the EU CBAM) spread globally, CLF's carbon-intensive production model could face pricing penalties in export markets.

The Bottom Line

CLF is a high-beta, cyclical stock that rewards investors who correctly call the steel cycle and penalizes those who are wrong. The tariff environment gives it a better-than-even shot at a meaningful 2026 recovery — a partial return toward 2022–2023 profitability is the most likely scenario if HRC prices stay above $800/ton.

The medium-term structural story is more challenging. The EAF competitive encroachment into automotive-grade steel and the gradual EV transition are secular headwinds that no amount of tariffs can permanently offset. CLF will need to either invest heavily in lower-carbon, lower-cost production (DRI-EAF hybrid strategy) or continue consolidating to achieve the scale needed to survive margin compression.

The single most important variable to watch is HRC steel pricing. If it stays above $850/ton with tariffs intact, the operating leverage math produces a profitable 2026. If HRC drifts back below $700/ton for any reason — trade deal, recession, import surge — the losses from 2024–2025 could return quickly. CLF's future is, as it has always been, a bet on steel.

Middletown Works — CLF's Flagship Steelmaking Facility

Located in Middletown, Ohio, this plant has produced steel continuously since 1899 — first as ARMCO, then AK Steel, and now Cleveland-Cliffs. It is CLF's single largest and most strategically important production site, capable of ~3 million tons of steel per year and serving as the heart of its automotive flat-rolled franchise. In 2025, a planned $1.3B green steel transformation was cancelled — leaving its long-term future in flux but its blast furnace operations ongoing.

History Timeline
1899 — The American Rolling Mill Company (ARMCO) Founded
George Matthew Verity co-founded ARMCO in Middletown, Ohio. The company's original mission was to produce a better quality, more consistent low-carbon steel for the emerging sheet metal industry. Middletown was chosen for its location on the Great Miami River with rail access and proximity to raw materials. Within a decade, ARMCO found its defining niche: supplying steel for the booming US automobile industry.
1910 — East Works Constructed
After a decade of rapid growth, a much larger integrated steel complex was built in southeastern Middletown at the end of South Crawford Street. Dubbed "East Works," this facility became the core of Middletown's steelmaking for generations. By the 1920s, ARMCO was pioneering the use of steel in automobile bodies, becoming a foundational supplier to Detroit.
1948 — Renamed Armco Steel Corporation
The company officially adopted the ARMCO acronym as its full name and expanded aggressively through the postwar manufacturing boom. Middletown Works underwent the "Project 600" expansion in the mid-1960s — estimated at $600M but ultimately costing nearly $1B — adding major blast furnace and rolling mill capacity to serve growing auto and appliance demand.
1989–1993 — Armco Merges with Kawasaki → Becomes AK Steel
Armco merged with Japan's Kawasaki Steel in 1989, and in 1993 the company renamed itself AK Steel Holdings (from Armco + Kawasaki). Headquarters moved from Middletown to Pittsburgh. The merger brought Japanese steelmaking technology and capital but also triggered workforce reductions that strained Middletown's economy — a dynamic chronicled in J.D. Vance's memoir Hillbilly Elegy, which drew on his family's ties to the plant.
1990s–2019 — AK Steel Era: Specialization in Automotive Grades
Under AK Steel, Middletown Works specialized in advanced high-strength steel (AHSS) and ultra-high-strength steel (UHSS) for automotive applications — grades like NEXMET®, ULTRALUME®, and proprietary electrical steels. By 2018, ~63% of AK Steel's total revenue came from automotive customers. Middletown was the hub of this franchise, producing hot-rolled, cold-rolled, galvanized, and other coated automotive-grade products. Periodic downturns, labor disputes, and import pressure made the years between 2000–2020 volatile for the facility.
March 13, 2020 — Cleveland-Cliffs Acquires AK Steel for ~$1.1B
CLF CEO Lourenco Goncalves immediately declared Middletown Works the "crown jewel" of the AK Steel portfolio. The acquisition closed the same week COVID-19 was declared a pandemic — challenging timing, but CLF held firm on integrating the facility. Middletown's 3M-ton blast furnace capacity and automotive customer relationships gave CLF the steel-producing backbone it needed to compete at scale. Combined with CLF's iron ore pellets from its Minnesota mines, full vertical integration was achieved for the first time.
2021–2022 — Supercycle: Middletown Running at Full Capacity
During the steel supercycle of 2021–2022, Middletown Works operated at or near full capacity. HRC prices near $1,900/ton meant massive margin per ton produced. The facility's hot strip mill — equipped with pair-cross rolling technology for superior shape and crown control — was running around the clock to fill automotive order books. This period validated CLF's acquisition thesis and funded rapid debt paydown at the corporate level.
2023 — Hydrogen Injection Trial + Green Steel Announcement
CLF completed a successful hydrogen injection trial at the Middletown blast furnace — injecting hydrogen as a partial replacement for natural gas in the tuyeres, reducing CO₂ emissions per ton. Building on this, CLF announced an ambitious $1.3B+ transformation: replacing the existing blast furnace with a 2.5 mtpa hydrogen-ready Direct Reduced Iron (DRI) plant and two 120-MW Electric Melting Furnaces (EMFs). The plan promised to cut CO₂ emissions by 50–90% while maintaining 3M ton annual output and adding 170 permanent and 1,200 construction jobs.
March 2024 — DOE Selects Middletown for $527M Grant
The U.S. Department of Energy selected the Middletown Works project for award negotiations — offering up to $527 million from the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law to fund the DRI/EMF conversion. This was one of the largest single DOE industrial decarbonization grants ever. Combined with CLF's own investment, total project cost exceeded $1.3B. Local officials, unions, and environmental groups broadly supported the plan, seeing it as a once-in-a-generation opportunity to modernize the mill and secure jobs for decades.
July 2025 — Project Cancelled. Grant Returned.
CLF informed the DOE it would not be pursuing the green steel project and forfeited the $527M grant. CEO Goncalves cited two key obstacles: (1) the unavailability of affordable low-carbon hydrogen at the scale required (~300,000 tons/year) — without it, a "hydrogen-ready" DRI plant would just be an expensive natural gas DRI plant with limited emissions benefit; and (2) the Trump administration had placed the DOE grant on a list of awards slated for termination, making the federal funding unreliable. The cancellation devastated local construction unions who had expected 1,200 jobs during the build phase.
2026 — Blast Furnace Continues. Future Under Review.
CEO Goncalves announced CLF is in conversations with the DOE to "revamp" the project concept — pivoting away from hydrogen toward a model centered on what he called "beautiful coal, beautiful coke, beautiful natural gas, our American iron ore from Minnesota." This signals a potential modernization of the existing blast furnace rather than a green technology replacement. Middletown Works remains CLF's flagship facility, its blast furnace operational, its ~2,500-person workforce intact, and its role as the automotive steel hub of the CLF system unchanged — for now.
What the Plant Produces
🔥 Ironmaking (Blast Furnace)
The blast furnace converts CLF's own iron ore pellets (mined in Minnesota) into molten pig iron using coke and natural gas. This is the foundation of the integrated steelmaking process — and the facility's highest CO₂-emitting step (~2 tons CO₂ per ton of iron). The BF feeds directly into the BOF.
⚗️ Steelmaking (Basic Oxygen Furnace)
Molten pig iron from the BF is combined with scrap steel in the BOF, where pure oxygen is blown in to burn off excess carbon and impurities. The result is molten steel with precisely controlled chemistry — critical for the exacting specifications of automotive-grade products. Multiple heats per day feed the caster.
🪨 Continuous Casting
Molten steel is continuously cast into slabs — thick, flat rectangular billets typically 8–10 inches thick and up to 60 inches wide. These slabs are the feedstock for the hot strip mill. Consistent slab quality is essential for downstream rolling uniformity.
🔁 Hot Strip Mill
Hot slabs are reheated and rolled down to thin coils of hot-rolled steel — gauges from ~1.5mm to 12mm. Middletown's hot strip mill uses pair-cross rolling technology for improved shape and crown control, producing flatter, more consistent coils than older mills. Hot-rolled coil is either sold directly or sent to cold rolling and finishing.
❄️ Cold Rolling & Finishing
Cold rolling further reduces thickness and improves surface finish and mechanical properties. Finishing lines apply coatings — galvanizing (zinc), aluminizing, or organic coatings — for corrosion protection. The finished products include exposed automotive body panels, structural members, door inners/outers, and underbody stampings destined for Ford, GM, Stellantis, Toyota, and Honda assembly plants.
🚗 Product Mix: Advanced Automotive Grades
Middletown produces the most demanding steel grades: Advanced High-Strength Steel (AHSS), Ultra-High-Strength Steel (UHSS), dual-phase grades, press-hardening steels, and exposed-quality grades for Class A surface applications. These products command premium pricing and require metallurgical expertise that EAF mini-mills are still working to replicate at scale.

What the Plan Would Have Done
Replaced the existing blast furnace with a 2.5 mtpa hydrogen-ready DRI plant (Direct Reduced Iron — using hydrogen or natural gas to reduce iron ore pellets into sponge iron without a blast furnace) feeding two 120-MW Electric Melting Furnaces. The existing BOF, caster, hot strip mill, and finishing lines would have been retained. CO₂ emissions cut by 50–90%. Total investment: $1.3B+. DOE contribution: $527M. CLF contribution: ~$800M+.
Why It Was Cancelled
1. No affordable hydrogen: Green hydrogen at scale remains expensive (~$3–5/kg vs. target of $1/kg). Without cheap H₂, the DRI plant would run on natural gas — cutting some emissions but not the 50–90% promised, undermining the entire premise. 2. Federal policy shift: The Trump administration's hostility to IRA/BIL-funded green energy projects put the $527M grant on a termination list, creating financing uncertainty CLF couldn't plan around. 3. Financial stress: CLF was losing money in 2024–2025 — spending $800M+ on a capital project was untenable.
What It Means for CLF's Future
The cancellation means Middletown Works will continue operating as a blast furnace facility for the foreseeable future — likely well into the 2030s. CLF's carbon footprint remains high relative to EAF competitors. The company cannot claim "green steel" credentials that some automotive OEMs are beginning to require from suppliers by 2030. CLF's competitive moat remains product quality and scale — not sustainability positioning. The blast furnace modernization talks with DOE could bring incremental improvements but no step-change in emissions.
Middletown's Role in CLF's Financial Story

Middletown Works is not just one plant among many — it is arguably the single most important asset in CLF's portfolio. At ~3 million tons annual capacity, it accounts for roughly 18–20% of CLF's total steelmaking output. Its automotive-grade product mix commands the highest selling prices across CLF's portfolio — typically $100–200/ton above commodity HRC — and its long-term supply agreements with the Big Three automakers provide the revenue floor that stabilizes CLF's earnings even in weak steel markets.

The facility was central to the 2021 supercycle earnings: running at full capacity with HRC prices near $1,900/ton, Middletown's operating leverage was extraordinary. Conversely, when steel prices fell and volumes dropped in 2024, idling blast furnaces at Middletown contributed directly to the quarterly losses.

Looking ahead, Middletown's fate is intertwined with two questions: (1) Can CLF maintain its automotive steel pricing premium as EAF mills like Nucor and SDI expand their automotive capabilities? And (2) Will CLF eventually be forced to invest in lower-carbon production to retain OEM customers who are setting aggressive Scope 3 emissions reduction targets? The cancelled green steel project was the answer to question two — its cancellation leaves that question unanswered.
